approach (reward) / avoid (threat) response

  • Social behavior is driven by desire to minimize threat and maximize reward
  • Social matters are most importance to use
    • in the brains default mode we mainly think about others and our relation to them
    • Social pain feels similar to physical pain - aspirin can reduce social pain
    • Social fear can be as bad as fear of death

Default reactions to threats

  • If uncontrolled, severe threats can cause default actions
  • Depending on the particular person those actions can either be
    • fight / attack
    • flee
    • freeze
  • Severly limits your cognitive resources
  • Learning from those situations while in fear is virtually impossible

SCARF

  • SCARF is a shorthand for a model of triggers that can generate approach or avoid responses
    • S: Status
    • C: Certainty
    • A: Autonomy
    • R: Relatedness
    • F: Fairness
  • Backed by and based on neuroscientistic findings
  • Basic paper: http://www.your-brain-at-work.com/files/NLJ_SCARFUS.pdf

How to actively improve social interactions

  • Try avoiding actions that triggering avoid responses
  • Try expanding actions that triggering reward responses
  • Raising awareness of social models and their importance

Dealing with social threats

If other people trigger threat responses, try...

  • Stating
    • Notice and speak out (silently) what your feeling is
    • E.g. "That makes me angry because it is unfair"
    • Will put you back in control
    • Works best if you can tell other people
    • Sometimes not powerful enough
  • Reappraisal
    • Find another interpretation for the situation
    • E.g. "He did not mean to be rude, he is just stressed out"
    • Much harder, requires much more brain power
    • Much more efficient

Prerequisite: Mindfulness

  • All these actions require almost permanent attention
  • Without noticing you have been threatened you can do nothing against it
  • You only have a very short time span until automated responses kick it
  • Mindfulness means to be intentionally in a situation without judgement, just observing
  • Being in the situation gives you the permanent attention
  • Can become a permanent mode if practiced over and over again
  • Science also tells us this also makes us healthier and happier
  • Dan Siegel on mindfulness
